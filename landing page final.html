Foreign ownership restrictions in Thailand

Thailand's Foreign Business Act (FBA)B.E. 2542 — limits foreign shareholding to 49% in restricted sectors across three annexes, with different degrees of restriction. generally limits foreign share ownership to 49% in restricted sectors. However, multiple pathways exist to achieve higher or full foreign ownership depending on sector and structure.

  • FBA-restricted business lists — Annexes 1, 2, and 3 define prohibited and restricted activities
  • Foreign Business License (FBL) pathway for majority ownership in approved cases
  • BOI-promoted status confers exemptions from FBA restrictions in eligible sectors
  • US-Thailand Treaty of Amity provides full ownership rights for US-national entities in most sectors
  • Nominee shareholder arrangements are illegal under Thai law and carry criminal penalties

Direct Answer
Under Thailand's Foreign Business Act (FBA)B.E. 2542 (1999) — the principal statute restricting foreign participation in Thai business activities across three annexes. B.E. 2542, foreign nationals are generally restricted to holding less than 50% of shares in a Thai company operating in restricted business sectors. However, several legal pathways exist to achieve majority or full foreign ownership depending on sector, structure, and regulatory approvals.
Why This Matters

Thailand permits foreign investment, but the FBA restricts majority foreign ownership across a broad range of business activities listed across three annexes. Annex 1 covers activities prohibited to foreigners absolutely. Annexes 2 and 3 cover restricted activities where majority foreign ownership requires Cabinet approval or a Foreign Business License respectively.

The key legal pathways to higher foreign ownership include: BOI promotion, which confers an exemption from FBA restrictions for eligible promoted activities; a Foreign Business License (FBL), which may permit majority ownership in Annex 2 and 3 sectors; and the US-Thailand Treaty of Amity, which allows US-national entities to hold majority ownership in most sectors not otherwise prohibited.

Key Considerations
  • ✓The 49% default threshold applies to FBA-restricted sectors — many service, trading, and commercial activities fall within Annex 3
  • ✓BOI promotion provides the most commercially practical pathway to majority or full foreign ownership for qualifying investment activities
  • ✓Nominee shareholder arrangements to circumvent the FBA are illegal under Thai law and expose investors to criminal liability
  • ✓Certain sectors (financial services, real estate, telecoms) have additional sector-specific ownership rules beyond the FBA
  • ✓Land ownership by foreign entities remains broadly restricted regardless of FBA status — separate rules apply

Direct Answer
Foreign investors in Thailand typically require company registration with the Department of Business Development (DBD), a Foreign Business License if operating in FBA-restricted sectors, and potentially BOI promotion for tax incentives and ownership exemptions. Sector-specific licences apply in financial services, energy, healthcare, and telecoms.
Why This Matters

The approval pathway is determined by three factors: the legal structure selected (company, branch, or representative office), the sector of activity, and whether the investor seeks BOI promotion. Each pathway has distinct timelines, capital requirements, and operational consequences.

BOI-promoted companies benefit from corporate income tax exemptions of 3–8 years, import duty reductions on machinery and raw materials, and — critically — an exemption from FBA foreign ownership restrictions for promoted activities. This makes BOI promotion the most commercially significant approval for many inbound investors.

Key Considerations
  • ✓DBD registration is required for all company formations — timelines range from 1 to 6 weeks depending on structure
  • ✓BOI applications require advance planning — eligibility assessment and documentation preparation precede formal submission
  • ✓The Eastern Economic Corridor (EEC) offers enhanced incentives for targeted industries including automotive, digital, and medical
  • ✓Financial services investors require SEC, BOT, or OIC licences depending on activity — separate from FBA and BOI processes
  • ✓Post-approval BOI reporting obligations must be maintained to preserve promoted status and incentives

Direct Answer
Key legal risks for foreign investors in Thailand include FBA non-compliance through nominee shareholder structures (criminal liability), unenforceable contract terms under Thai law, land ownership restrictions, foreign exchange repatriation constraints, and sector-specific regulatory exposure. Proactive pre-entry legal due diligence substantially reduces these risks.
Why This Matters

Legal risk in Thailand often materialises retrospectively — after capital is committed, a structure is formed, or an acquisition is completed. Many risks are invisible without jurisdiction-specific legal review, including restrictions embedded in land title, limitations on profit repatriation under Bank of Thailand rules, or enforceability gaps in agreements drafted without Thai law consideration.

For acquisitions, due diligence on Thai targets must include regulatory licence status, shareholder agreement enforceability, and historical FBA compliance. Undisclosed nominee arrangements in the target company can create liability exposure for the acquirer.

Key Considerations
  • ✓FBA compliance in existing structures must be verified — prior nominee arrangements create ongoing criminal exposure
  • ✓Land ownership by foreign entities is broadly restricted under the Land Code — leasehold and condominium structures require specialist advice
  • ✓Foreign exchange controls affect how profits, dividends, and capital are repatriated — Bank of Thailand documentation requirements apply
  • ✓Joint venture agreements must be reviewed for enforceability — Thai courts apply local conflict of law analysis
  • ✓Labour law compliance for mixed Thai-foreign workforces involves specific work permit, visa, and employment contract requirements

Direct Answer
Setting up a foreign-owned company in Thailand involves five sequential stages: structure and ownership analysis, Department of Business Development (DBD) registration, FBA compliance assessment, BOI application where applicable, and operational readiness including work permits and banking. End-to-end timelines range from 6 weeks for a straightforward Thai majority company to 6–9 months where BOI promotion is sought.
Why This Matters

The formation timeline is not linear for every investor. Structure selection directly determines which approval processes are triggered — a BOI-promoted company requires a parallel application track that adds months but unlocks ownership and tax benefits that fundamentally change the commercial case. Decisions made at incorporation cannot easily be reversed without cost and regulatory exposure.

Foreign investors frequently underestimate the lead time for BOI applications and sector-specific licences, leading to operational delays and capital sitting idle. Engaging counsel before structure selection — not after — allows parallel processing and realistic timeline management from day one.

Key Considerations
  • ✓DBD registration for a Thai Limited Company typically takes 1–3 weeks; a Foreign Business License application adds 2–4 months on top
  • ✓BOI applications require pre-submission eligibility assessment and documentation preparation — formal approval takes 2–6 months from submission
  • ✓Minimum registered capital requirements vary: THB 2M for most companies, THB 3M for FBA-licensed entities, and higher thresholds for BOI-promoted companies depending on promotion tier
  • ✓Work permit and visa processing for foreign management runs in parallel and typically takes 4–8 weeks after company registration
  • ✓Common delays include incomplete shareholder documentation, DBD name reservation rejections, and BOI queries on business plan projections
  • ✓Corporate bank account opening has become more stringent — plan for 4–8 weeks and prepare comprehensive KYC documentation in advance

Direct Answer
M&A transactions in Thailand for foreign buyers follow a structured lifecycle: deal structuring (share purchase vs asset acquisition), legal and regulatory due diligence, FBA compliance verification, Share Purchase Agreement (SPA) or asset transfer documentation, regulatory filings including DBD and sector-specific notifications, and post-closing integration and compliance. Foreign ownership restrictions in the target company must be assessed before any binding commitment is made.
Why This Matters

Thailand M&A presents specific legal risks that are not present in other jurisdictions. A target company may carry historical FBA non-compliance — including undisclosed nominee shareholder arrangements — that transfers liability to the acquirer on completion. BOI-promoted status in the target may not automatically transfer and requires BOI notification or re-application. Land assets held by the target are subject to separate ownership restrictions that may require divestment or restructuring as a condition of the deal.

Deal structure selection between share purchase and asset acquisition has significant consequences for FBA exposure, tax treatment, employment obligations, and the treatment of existing licences and regulatory approvals. This decision requires early-stage legal input before heads of terms are agreed.

Key Considerations
  • ✓Share acquisitions transfer the target's full regulatory history — including any FBA violations or undisclosed nominee structures — to the acquirer
  • ✓Asset acquisitions avoid historical liability but require fresh regulatory licences and employment arrangements in the acquiring entity
  • ✓Thailand does not currently have a mandatory merger control regime for most sectors, but financial services, telecoms, and energy transactions require sector regulator notification
  • ✓BOI-promoted status in the target company does not automatically transfer — BOI notification and approval is required to preserve incentives post-acquisition
  • ✓Land ownership held by a foreign-majority target after acquisition may constitute an FBA violation — pre-closing restructuring is often required
  • ✓SPA governing law and dispute resolution mechanics must be carefully structured — Thai courts apply local conflict of law analysis to foreign-law agreements
